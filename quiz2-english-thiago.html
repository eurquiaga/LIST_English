Topic 1 — Newspaper Report
Features, headlines, facts & quotations
▼
What is a Newspaper Report?
A newspaper report is a non-fiction text that informs readers about real events. It uses special features to grab the reader's attention and present facts clearly.
Key Features
📌 Headline
Big bold title. Catchy! Uses powerful verbs or alliteration.
"Robot Dog Joins Local School!"
📝 Subheading
Extra detail below the headline. Gives more information.
"Students welcome new helper"
✍️ Byline
Who wrote it, when, and which newspaper.
"By Sam Rivera | 23 June 2026"
💬 Quotation
Real words from a real person. Makes it trustworthy.
"We are thrilled!" said Mrs Collins.
✅ Facts
Things that can be checked and proven true.
"The robot weighs 3 kg."
📦 Fact Box
Extra quick facts on the side. Easy to read at a glance.
Facts vs Opinions
⚠️ Opinion (NOT a fact)
✗ "It was the most beautiful garden in the world."
✗ "Everyone loved the robot."
✅ Fact (can be checked!)
✓ "The garden covers 300 square metres."
✓ "The robot learned five new commands."

Topic 2 — Overused Words
Upgrade your vocabulary!
▼
What are Overused Words?
Overused words are words that writers use too often. They are boring and vague. Good writers replace them with precise, interesting alternatives that paint a clearer picture for the reader.
Common Overused Words & Better Alternatives
❌ Overused → ✅ Better alternatives
good → excellent, brilliant, outstanding
big → enormous, gigantic, vast
nice → welcoming, pleasant, delightful
happy → delighted, thrilled, overjoyed
interesting → captivating, fascinating, gripping
said → explained, exclaimed, whispered
looked → stared, glanced, gazed
fun → thrilling, exhilarating, entertaining
See the difference!
❌ Before — boring & vague
"Yesterday was a good day. We visited a big museum. The guide was nice and I felt happy."
✅ After — precise & engaging
"Yesterday was an outstanding day. We visited an enormous museum. The guide was welcoming and I felt delighted."
💡 Remember
Think of words like colours on a palette. More specific words make your writing more colourful and clear! But don't use a fancy word if you don't know what it means. 🎨

Topic 3 — Who Am I Writing To?
Adapting language to your audience
▼
What is Audience Awareness?
Your audience is the person (or people) you are writing for. Different audiences need different language and style. A message to your friend sounds very different from a message to your teacher!
The 5 Audiences
👫 Friend
Informal · casual · exclamation marks · emoji · short messages
"OMG I forgot my book again!! Can you help?? 😅"
👩‍🏫 Teacher
Polite · formal · complete sentences · Dear/Thank you
"Dear Mrs Smith, I would like to ask about my homework. Thank you."
👨‍👩‍👧 Parents
Clear · organised · dates & details · informative
"Please sign the permission slip by Friday. The trip begins at 8:30 a.m."
🧒 Younger Children
Simple words · short sentences · direct · positive reminders
"Walk slowly! Keep your hands by your sides. Be kind!"
🌍 General Public
No personal language · facts · clear and concise · formal
"The library will be closed on Monday 28 June."
Same situation — different audience!
Situation: "You forgot your homework."
👫 To a Friend
"Ugh, I forgot my homework AGAIN!! Did you remember yours?? 😬"
👩‍🏫 To a Teacher
"Dear Mrs Smith, I am sorry to inform you that I forgot my homework today. I will bring it tomorrow. Thank you for your understanding."
🔑 Key clues to identify the audience
Look for: greetings (Dear/Hi/Hey), tone (formal/informal), vocabulary (simple/complex), punctuation (!!!/.), and purpose (inform/instruct/chat).
📰 Newspaper Report

Read the article, then answer the questions

A newspaper report has: headline, subheading, byline, facts, quotations, and photos with captions.

Rubber Connects People Around the World
This useful material helps make tyres, shoes, gloves and many everyday products
By Daniel Cruz, Junior Reporter  |  20 June 2026  |  MATERIAL WORLD NEWS
Latex from rubber trees is collected and turned into many useful products.

Every day, millions of people use products made from rubber. From the tyres on our cars to the gloves doctors wear, rubber plays an important role in our lives. But have you ever wondered where rubber comes from and how it connects people around the world?

From Tree to Product
Natural rubber comes from the latex of rubber trees. Farmers carefully collect the latex by making a small cut in the tree bark. The liquid is then collected in cups. The latex is cleaned, mixed and processed into rubber. Major rubber-producing countries include Thailand, Indonesia, Vietnam, India and Malaysia.

Rubber is used to make many items we use every day. These include tyres, shoes, gloves, playground surfaces, sports equipment and medical supplies.

"Rubber is one of the most useful materials because it is strong, flexible and waterproof. Many products we use every day would not be possible without it." – explained materials scientist Dr Patel.

Experts report that recycling rubber products can reduce waste and protect the environment. Old tyres can be reused to make playground floors, sports tracks and roads.

📦 Fact Box Main raw ingredient: Latex (sap) from rubber trees
Main producing countries: Thailand, Indonesia, Vietnam, India, Malaysia
Interesting fact: A single rubber tree can produce latex for about 25 to 30 years!
